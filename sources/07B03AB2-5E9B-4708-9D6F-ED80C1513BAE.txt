A Multilevel Analysis of Factors Associated with Secondary School Teachers’ Perceived Digital Competence

Hulya Avci, Department of Educational Psychology, Texas A&M University, College Station, TX, USA, hulya.avci@tamu.edu

Abstract: This study aims to examine the relationship between perceived digital competence and potential influencing factors among secondary school teachers. Data were drawn from the OECD’s Teaching and Learning International Survey 2018 U.S. national teacher survey of 165 schools and 2,414 secondary teachers. A two-level multilevel linear modeling was conducted to investigate the impact of (a) teachers' pre- and in-service ICT training, job satisfaction, and collegial collaboration in the teacher level and (b) collective teacher innovativeness, school innovativeness, and digital infrastructure in the school level. Results revealed several factors, including ICT training, collegial collaboration, and digital infrastructure, significantly predicted secondary teachers' perceived digital competence within and across schools. These findings are essential for understanding the multi-level aspects of digital competence and the potential for implementing interventions to fortify teacher training programs and promote collaborative efforts that can help cultivate a resilient and interconnected educational community.

Introduction
The advance of information and communication technology (ICT) has transformed industrial societies into knowledge-based ones, as acknowledged in various studies (Hatlevik, 2010; OECD, 2011). Thus, numerous countries (e.g., Australia, Belgium, Hong Kong, Norway, the US) and international entities such as the European Union (EU), the International Society for Technology in Education (ISTE), and UNESCO, have made efforts to foster 21st-century skills. These initiatives emphasize the pivotal role of teachers' digital competence in enhancing students' digital literacy in the knowledge society (Fosnacht, 2020; Wu et al., 2022).
Digital competence is paramount for addressing digitalization challenges (Ilomäki et al., 2016). Hence, the significance of teachers' digital competence has grown, enabling them to efficiently incorporate digital technologies in their classrooms and enhance students' learning experiences (Krumsvik, 2014). However, there remains a lack of consistency in identifying the essential knowledge, skills, and attitudes that teachers need for technology-enhanced teaching. Wu et al. (2022) argued that it is crucial to explore various factors associated with teachers' digital competence to gain a deeper understanding of digital competence.
Previous studies focused on individual factors such as age, gender, teaching experience, and subject taught (e.g., Cattaneo et al., 2022; Guillén-Gámez et al., 2021) while others examined contextual factors including students' digital access, digital infrastructure, professional development (Cattaneo et al., 2022), school location (Vitanova et al., 2015), network infrastructure, school facilitation, curriculum facilitation, and peer influence on technology use (Lucas et al., 2021). Additionally, several studies examined the association between teachers' attitudes toward the use of technology and student outcomes (e.g., Lorenz et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2022). Regarding my systematic literature review of K-12 teachers’ digital competence (Avci & Pedersen, 2023), to the best of my knowledge, few studies have investigated multi-level aspects of teachers’ digital competence both at the teacher and school levels (Claro et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2022). Therefore, there is a need for a more comprehensive investigation to thoroughly assess the extent of teachers' digital competence.
This study examines secondary teachers’ perceived digital competence through the predictors of (a) teachers’ ICT-related pre-and in-service training, job satisfaction, and collegial collaboration in the teacher level and (b) collective innovativeness, school innovativeness, and digital infrastructure in the school level. Unlike studies that examined factors concerning demographics or school-related factors, this study focused on unstable individual and contextual factors such as collaboration, job satisfaction, innovativeness, and other factors that are potentially linked to teachers' digital competence. The findings of this study will advance an understanding of large-scale responses from secondary teachers clustered within U.S. schools on multiple factors influencing their perceived digital competence. The present study aims to address the following research questions:
RQ1.Which teacher-level factors (pre- and in-service ICT training, job satisfaction, and collegial collaboration) predict teachers’ perceived digital competence after controlling for teacher socio- demographics?

RQ2.Which school-level factors (collective innovativeness, school innovativeness, and digital infrastructure) predict teachers’ perceived digital competence after controlling for school conditions?
RQ3.What is the extent to which teachers’ perceived digital competence is related to both teacher-level and school-level random effects?
Conceptual framework
This study considered several key variables based on existing literature. These variables include teachers' involvement in ICT-focused pre-service and in-service training, job satisfaction, collegial collaboration among teachers, collective teacher innovativeness, school-level innovativeness, and digital infrastructure within and across the schools. Specifically, the outcome variable, teachers' perceived digital competence, revolved around two key aspects of digital skills outlined in the European Framework for the Digital Competence of Educators (Redecker, 2017). The first aspect is "teaching and learning," which relates to teachers' self-efficacy in using ICT to support student learning. The second aspect is "empowering learners," which involves helping students utilize digital technologies for educational purposes, such as using ICT for class projects. This nested design of the study aligns with Bronfenbrenner's Socio-Ecological Model (SEM, 1979), which characterizes the ecological environment as a nested structure of interconnected systems. This structure corresponds to the organization of the study's data, where teachers are grouped within schools. The SEM consists of four key systems: microsystem, mesosystem, exosystem, and macrosystem, all centered around an individual (Bronfenbrenner, 1979). In this context, the teacher level is part of the microsystem, the lowest level in the hierarchy, while the school level is part of the mesosystem, where microsystems nest. Hence, this framework allows us to explore and grasp various influences and contexts beyond the individual level as shown in Figure 1.

Methodology
Data source
This study employed data from the Teaching and Learning International Survey (TALIS) of 2018, which is an international, large-scale survey of lower secondary teachers and school principals in public and private schools across about 48 countries and economies (OECD, 2019). Particularly, data for this study were drawn from the TALIS 2018 U.S. national public-use teacher and principal survey data retrieved from the National Center for Education Statistics (NCES) website. U.S. TALIS 2018 data are detached from the international merged data set to conduct multilevel modeling analysis teacher survey responses constitute the teacher-level (Level 1) data file and principal survey responses partially constitute the school-level (Level 2) data file.

Sample
Up to 20 teachers were randomly sampled from within each school which yielded a total of 165 lower-secondary schools and 2,600 teachers working in the sampled U.S. schools that participated in the survey. After accounting

for missing values, this study sample consisted of a total of 165 schools and 2,414 teachers teaching 7th, 8th, and 9th grades with a variety of subjects.

Measures
For the outcome variable, I conducted a thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006) of the variables and items that are related to ICT knowledge and skills in the TALIS teacher questionnaire (OECD, 2019). Using previous research on digital competence, I crafted an alignment matrix of teachers’ digital competence which consists of a cluster of the relevant items from the questionnaire according to (a) the Digital Competence Framework for Educators (Redecker & Punie, 2017) and (b) UNESCO ICT Competence Framework for Teachers (UNESCO, 2018). The best indicators of teachers’ digital competence were identified in accordance with the alignment matrix: (1) “Let students use ICT (Information and Communication Technology) for projects or class work” and
(2) “Support student learning through the use of digital technology (e.g., computers, tablets, smart boards)” (OECD, 2019, pp. 19–23). Reliability analysis of the items comprising the dependent variable yielded a good Cronbach’s Alpha, 끫뷸 = .69, a high composite reliability score of .86, and AVE of .76.
This study comprised several teacher and school-level control variables measured by a four-point Likert scale ranging from strongly agree (4) to strongly disagree (1). In the teacher level, I controlled for teacher characteristics that might influence the digital competence of the teacher in relation to the other independent variables regarding the findings of the previous studies (e.g., Guillén-Gámez et al., 2021; Krumsvik et al., 2016). These include teacher age, gender, and overall teaching experience in the teacher level; school types (private or public), and school location in the school level.

Analytical strategy
This study applied a two-level Hierarchical Linear Modeling (HLM) approach to examine the relationship between teacher- and school-level factors and teachers' perceived digital competence. HLM offers several benefits in educational research, including accounting for the nested structure of educational data and distinguishing between variations within and between schools (Raudenbush & Bryk, 2002). The analysis used the default maximum likelihood estimation in STATA 18 (StataCorp, 2023). To identify significant predictors at both levels and determine the most parsimonious model fitting the data, this study followed the step-up approach which means beginning with a null model excluding predictors (Raudenbush & Bryk, 2002).
The data analysis process comprised three models: (1) a fully unconditional (null) model utilizing 'one- way ANOVA with random effects,' (2) a partially conditional model employing 'random coefficient regression model,' and (3) a fully conditional model using 'intercepts and slopes as outcomes multilevel' analysis. Group mean centering was applied to all level-1 predictors to focus on teacher-level effects on the outcome variable, while grand mean centering was used for all level-2 predictors as suggested by Enders and Tofighi (2007).
The initial HLM model was the unconditional or null model which did not include any Level 1 and Level 2 predictors. The purpose of this model was to compute the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) to obtain the initial partitioning of the total variability that occurs at the teacher and school levels of the analyses (Raudenbush & Bryk, 2002). ICC was found .081, justifying the use of HLM in this study. The equations for each model are displayed in the following.

Model 1: Fully unconditional model
As the initial step in the multilevel analysis, a fully unconditional model was carried out to ascertain the presence of significant variance in the outcome variable among schools. It was found that 17% of the overall variance in teachers' perceived digital competence was between schools, while the remaining 83% was within schools (ICC
= .174, SE = .022, 95% CI = .14 – .22). The between-school variance was found significant, indicating that teachers’ perceived digital competence ratings varied significantly across schools. The mean teachers' perceived digital competence across all schools was found 끫뷺 = 2.95, SE = .02, p < .001, indicating a high value on this variable. Consequently, it was appropriate to proceed with the subsequent partially conditional model to further investigate the teacher-level predictors contributing to the variance in teachers' perceived digital competence between schools.

Model 2: Teacher-level factors
Teacher-level predictors including pre-service ICT training, in-service ICT training, teacher job satisfaction and collegial collaboration, were added to the baseline Model 1. All these variables proposed that they would significantly contribute to teachers’ perceived digital competence variation between schools, respectively. The HLM results revealed that after controlling for teachers’ age, gender, and teaching experience, the estimated coefficients for pre-service ICT training, in-service ICT training, and teacher job satisfaction were statistically significant, which means that a one-unit increase in pre-service ICT training, in-service ICT training, and collegial collaboration would predict .14-, .19-, and .10-point increases in teachers’ perceived digital competence levels (끫뷺
= .138, SE = .039; 끫뷺 = .193, SE = .051; 끫뷺 = .095, SE = .036). However, teacher job satisfaction did not support the relevant proposed hypothesis (p = .48). Furthermore, model fit through the deviance statistic was measured by –2 Log Likelihood (–2LL) and showed that model improvement from Model 1 to Model 2 was significant (끫룊2
= 161899.2, df = 10, p = .000).

Model 3: School-level factors
The school-level predictors including collective innovativeness, school innovativeness, and digital infrastructure were added to Model 2. The HLM results showed that the deviance was reduced by a significant amount (끫룊2 = 42618.7, df = 13, p = .000), indicating that the addition of those school-level predictors accounted for significant variation in teachers’ perceived digital competence levels. Thus, the model improvement revealed that the final model, namely Model 3, provided the best fit among the models. The multilevel analysis of the final model also showed that the between-schools variance decreased by 5.6% (from 10.7 to 10.1) moving from Model 2 and the between-teachers-within-school variance decreased by 5.9% (from 47.8% to 41.9%). The comparison of the likelihood ratio test for the three models is displayed in Table 3.

The overall results of the multilevel analysis showed that the estimated coefficients for the independent variables including pre-service ICT training, in-service ICT training, collegial collaboration, and digital infrastructure had a significant relationship with teachers’ perceived digital competence across schools (끫뷺 = .139, SE = .041; 끫뷺 = .204, SE = .054; 끫뷺 = .114, SE = .037), except the variables of job satisfaction, collective innovativeness and school innovativeness (p = .48, p = .44, p = .98). As a result, a one-unit increase in pre-service ICT training, in-service ICT training, collegial collaboration, and would predict .14-, .20-, and .11-point increases in teachers’ perceived digital competence levels, respectively (p < .001, p < .01). Contrary to digital infrastructure, a one-unit increase in digital infrastructure would predict a .12-point decrease in teachers’ perceived digital competence levels (끫뷺 = –.116, SE = .040, p < .01).

Discussion
The multilevel analysis of teachers' digital competence revealed several key findings. First, secondary teachers who participated in the pre-service and in-service ICT training indicated higher perceived digital competence, emphasizing the importance of professional development training programs in enhancing teachers' digital skills. Similarly, Mendeley (2009) found that professional development and ICT usage were antecedents of ICT competency. A recent study (Chin et al., 2022) showed that teachers’ pedagogical and information technology skills are influenced by their previous professional development experiences. Second, collegial collaboration among teachers positively and significantly affected their digital competence as highlighted by empirical studies (e.g., Røkenes et al., 2022; Shah, 2012). This outcome is also consistent with another study that found a positive correlation between teachers' collegial collaboration and classroom ICT usage (Hatlevik & Hatlevik, 2018). Although I controlled for certain demographic factors, such as age and gender, they played a role in teachers’ perceived digital competence. As such, the negative association between teaching experience and teachers’ perceived digital competence indicated that more experienced teachers tend to have lower perception of their digital competencies. This suggests a need for tailored ICT training for experienced teachers. However, school- level factors including collective innovativeness and school innovativeness, did not significantly influence teachers’ perceived digital competence while digital infrastructure had a significant outcome.
The findings of this study could provide valuable insights into the determinants of teachers' digital competence as well as holding particular relevance to the ISLS conference theme of "Learning as a cornerstone of healing, resilience, and community." In today's rapidly evolving educational landscape, teachers’ digital competencies are instrumental in building a resilient and interconnected educational community. The positive impact of ICT training highlights the significance of formal education in preparing teachers for the digital era. Thus, as individuals learn and adapt to heal and thrive in their lives, teachers should continuously learn and adapt to effectively integrate digital technologies for the benefit of their students’ learning and digital literacy. Furthermore, the positive impact of collegial collaboration on teachers' perceived digital competence underscores the importance of a supportive professional community. This collaborative aspect aligns with the theme of "community" and suggests the healing and growth potential of shared learning experiences.
Implications for further research
This study suggests practical implications for teacher training programs and school support of building digital infrastructure. Emphasizing ICT training during the initial teacher education and beyond, encouraging collaboration among teachers, and tailoring training for experienced educators can enhance teachers' digital competencies and equip them to effectively navigate the digital transformation at schools. School-level factors focusing on innovativeness while not significant in this context, should not be disregarded, as digital infrastructure improvements at schools directly support teachers’ digital competence. Further research might be needed to delve deeper into the intricacies of the innovativeness factor and other individual or contextual factors at both teacher and school levels. All in all, this study offers a comprehensive understanding of the multi-level aspects of digital competence among teachers, paving the way for more effective collective learning practices where teachers and teacher educators share knowledge, experiences, and innovative practices in the digital era.
