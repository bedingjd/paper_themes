Mapping the Complexity of Learning: A Learning Experience Network Analysis Approach for Iterative Design Moves

Jonan Phillip Donaldson, University of Alabama at Birmingham, jonandonaldson@uab.edu Ahreum Han, University of Southern California, arnova09@gmail.com
Shulong Yan, University of California, Davis, shlyan@ucdavis.edu Seiyon Lee, University of Florida, seiyonly@gmail.com
Sean Kao, Texas A&M University, sean.kao@tamu.edu
Abstract: This methodological paper interrogates the potential of network analysis within the design-based research (DBR) framework for improving the design of learning experiences. We present the learning experience network analysis (LENA) approach for analyzing learner- generated data about their experiences in learning activities. Utilizing a complex systems perspective, this study reveals how network analysis elucidates intricate patterns of interdependencies between experiences of struggles, aspects that worked well, and alignment of their experiences with principles from learning theories. Those patterns can be used to construct new design moves for the subsequent DBR iteration. Our findings underscore the capacity of this method to capture the dynamism and complexity inherent in learning environments, thereby informing more adaptive and responsive design strategies. This work extends the methodological toolkit available for DBR, advocating for a more complex, situated understanding of the ways in which the data-informed design can shape the learning experience from the learner’s perspective.

Introduction and background
This methodological paper presents our work towards development of an analytical approach within the context of design-based research (DBR) studies focusing on the design of learning experiences. Specifically, we developed an approach for analyzing learner-produced qualitative data through network analysis in a way that honors the complexity of learning and results in design changes for subsequent iterations.
DBR has become a signature methodology within the field of learning sciences (Hoadley, 2018; Barab, 2006). In DBR, researchers and practitioners collaborate to iteratively design, develop, implement, and evaluate learning experiences and environments. DBR allows for concurrent development of both theory and practical design principles, thereby helping to bridge the traditional divide between educational theory and practice (Campanella & Penuel, 2021).
One ongoing challenge in DBR is determining how to make systematic principled design changes between iterations of each implementation cycle. Often, these design decisions are heavily guided by the past experiences and intuitive expertise of the researchers and practitioners involved, rather than being driven by systematic data analysis. Therefore, innovations in analytical methods and tools are needed to facilitate more reflective conversation between researcher and design (Schön, 1992), while also reducing potential issues like research bias and reactivity that can emerge when researchers overly rely on selective curation of data to fit their own perspectives and assumptions (Hammersley & Atkinson, 2007; Maxwell, 2012).
Since its origins, the field of learning sciences has strongly focused on designs for learning, specifically through design research methodologies that investigate how educational designs are practically experienced by learners and teachers, and how these lived experiences in turn relate back to underlying learning theories (Brown, 1992; Collins, 1992). Learning theories aim to produce guiding design principles grounded in research, while systematic analysis of learner experiences helps further develop those theories. However, truly embracing the inherent complexity and contextual nature of learning highlights the need for more expansive theoretical frameworks and holistic methodological approaches (diSessa & Cobb, 2004) that enable researchers to fully comprehend the dynamic interplay between theory and practice as learning designs are implemented.
Aligned with these commitments to embracing the messy complexity of authentic learning, we argue there is a need for new and innovative data analysis methods tailored for generating principled, data-grounded design refinements within DBR studies. In this paper, we describe our initial formulation of one such method that leverages network analysis techniques to map the complex interdependencies between learner experiences of challenges, aspects in which learners had positive experiences, and alignment with learning theory principles.
Network analysis techniques have been incorporated into DBR studies in various ways (Barany et al., 2021; Gomez, 2021). In general, network analysis provides tools to visually and statistically examine and quantify the structures of interrelationship within complex systems (Borgatti et al., 2009; Luke & Harris, 2007),

representing entities or components as nodes and their connections as links (or edges) between nodes. Network analysis has become widely used across education, medicine, biology, and other domains to surface unseen patterns within complex phenomena. Three types commonly used in education research are: social network analysis (SNA - Scott, 2017), semantic network analysis (SemNA - Christensen & Kenett, 2021), and epistemic network analysis (ENA - Shaffer et al., 2016). these However, none of them has typically been employed as the primary analytical driver of design refinements between study iterations. For example, SNA might reveal insights about learner collaborative patterns, but these insights are difficult to translate directly into design modifications. Similarly, ENA can help DBR researchers understand changes in epistemic frames, but again it remains difficult to translate such findings directly into a set of design moves. Therefore, we identified a need for a tailored network analysis approach specifically focused on guiding informed design decision-making from data generated from authentic learning experience.
To address the gap, we propose learning experience network analysis (LENA) as a new approach, in which each node represents a particular type of learner experience across three key categories: learner struggles, aspects of the design that learners experienced as working particularly well, and experiences aligning to theoretical principles. The network mapping and cluster analysis of relationships between these experience nodes helps systematically unpack the complex interdependencies at play in how a learning design is holistically experienced. This methodological innovation provides structured guidance for refining designs based on learner experience data, complementing other DBR approaches like ENA (Shaffer et al., 2016) that focus on modeling the nature of learning itself. Our research questions aim to explore how LENA can illuminate the complex interplay of learner experiences, design elements, and theoretical principles within DBR studies. We further seek to demonstrate how insights gleaned from LENA contribute to the iterative refinement of learning designs, enhancing their effectiveness and alignment with learning theories. Our goal is to advance an analytical lens to the DBR methodological toolkit that embraces the contextualized complexity of learning in practice.

Learning experience network analysis
DBR necessitates analytical techniques that incorporate the intricacy of learning experiences while not being overly complicated for researchers to employ. LENA progressed through various design-based research undertakings across an array of educational contexts. The current formalization of LENA materialized by way of a workshop and subsequent teamwork between the workshop facilitators and attendees, and DBR research projects in the teaching and learning communities in higher education
The LENA methodology encompasses: qualitative data collection, qualitative data coding, conducting correlation analysis, creating a network map, conducting cluster analysis, analyzing betweenness centrality values, and leveraging aspects that worked and alignment with principles from theory to formulate design moves to address struggles (see figure 1).

Development of LENA
During the process of LENA model development, our objective was to cultivate analytical methodologies for DBR investigations that illuminate the complexity inherent in learning and introduce new ways for researchers to generate data-informed design changes. Learning settings and the very nature of learning encompass numerous intricate systems interacting at nested levels of interdependent complex systems. The way learners experience the learning designs we construct in DBR projects entails countless types of experiences and interpretations of those experiences which form the mutually dependent components of a complex system. Network analysis enables expansive, holistic approaches to grasping connections between learning experiences. LENA is solely concerned with learner experiences since its purpose is to inform future iterations of learning experience designs in DBR studies. We aim to leverage data that represents learners’ experiences as they narrated straightforwardly, leaving minimal room for the need for interpretation by the researchers.
Our initial focus was on inspecting network maps of correlated struggles and aspects that students experienced as working well in order to capitalize on what was working effectively to remedy struggles. For instance, in some studies this analysis assisted us in devising design modifications to address matters regarding collaboration, relevance, and framing. In another DBR investigation of an argumentation activity, the analysis culminated in design moves encompassing broadening the range of topics, furnishing additional sources of information, and providing more explicit guidance pertaining to expectations. During a pre-conference workshop along with work in subsequent undertakings, we realized that design moves in DBR should be rooted not solely in evidence from the specific context but also in principles from learning theories. As a result, we now construct network maps that encompass aspects that students construed as beneficial or problematic as well as components of their experiences that align with principles from the learning theories underpinning the design.
There are several considerations for the use of LENA. First, LENA may not be appropriate for all DBR investigations. This methodology is intended for probing learners’ experiences engaging with a learning design, rather than other realms of DBR such as design-based implementation research or systems-level design research. To underscore the validity of using learner narratives as data, we draw upon constructivist and sociocultural learning theories, which posit that learners’ articulations of their experiences are not merely reflective but constitutive of their learning processes (Danish & Gresalfi, 2018; Kafai, 2006). This approach aligns with the notion that learners construct knowledge not in isolation but through the prism of their experiences and interactions, thereby making learner narratives a rich data source for understanding complex learning dynamics. Second, it works optimally when there are at least ten participants, since fewer makes constructing meaningful network maps difficult given that LENA depends on code co-occurrence. Theoretical saturation frequently transpires with more than twelve participants according to prominent guidelines. Third, it is also imperative that the context enables compiling rich qualitative information from learners about their experiences. Although there is no standard quantitative gauge for judging qualitative richness, we deem data to be rich when it consists of multiple detailed paragraphs furnished in response to questions expressly targeting learner experiences.
Application of LENA
Various data types have different affordances and constraints in capturing learner experiences in terms of expediency, levels of granularity, and directness of participants’ interpretation. In higher education course settings, for example, we found individual reflection papers to be optimal; however, coding these reflections required months thus precluding implementation of tweaks between semesters. Surveys swiftly compiled perceptions but lacked depth. Interviews furnished ample detail but were time-intensive to conduct and analyze. Video recordings of learning activities informed other forms of analysis but didn’t adequately capture learners’ own interpretations of their experiences. Therefore, we suggest researchers should try out different qualitative data types to strike the right balance between authenticity and expediency. In our own DBR studies, we prioritize authentic representations necessitating minimal interpretation by the researchers because many in our teams were novice DBR researchers and did not have backgrounds or training in human subjects research or working with learning theories. We aim to leverage data that represents learners' experiences as they are reported by learners, leaving minimal room for the need for interpretation by the researchers. By focusing on learners' direct accounts, we aim to capture their authentic interpretations of their learning experiences, which are central to the goals of design-based research.
LENA: Data coding
We typically code at the sentence level, though the researcher must establish the most fitting granularity based on the specific context. For example, in some cases the paragraph level has been more appropriate. The number of

codes varies by study, but generally we find that the theory category requires around 3 to 8 a priori codes per theory. The struggles and what worked categories often necessitate 20 to 50 emergent codes each. We attempt to code every sentence, except those irrelevant to our investigation. Sometimes multiple codes fit a single sentence, especially codes representing principles from different theories that overlap conceptually. Therefore, we suggest using open-ended questions to gather richer and more diverse datasets for investigating learning experiences.
A vital initial step in DBR is grounding the learning design in appropriate theories. Various facets of the theories guiding the design are translated into design principles steering concrete design choices. Accordingly, when preparing the codebook, one category encompasses all the theory-derived principles. The principles being coded mirror those applied in originally engineering the learning experience. Learner experiences are coded for indications of alignment with those precise principles. Throughout the remained of this section, we will give examples from one of the author’s DBR studies that focused on generative game-based learning (GBL) where students worked in groups to create Dungeons & Dragons style games for a university literature course. In this study, the a priori theory codes reflected principles from cognitive constructivist theory, social constructivist theory, constructionist theory, self-determination theory, situated learning theory, transformative learning theory, and the dynamical systems model of role identity.
We also aim to pinpoint what elements of the design were problematic and could potentially be enhanced in subsequent iterations. We can identify these aspects by coding learner experiences that denote struggles impeding productive learning. The researcher should be especially attentive in order to distinguish struggles that are fruitful for learning from those that actively hinder it. Only detrimental struggles warrant coding as such, while productive struggles like those producing cognitive dissonance align with codes stemming from relevant learning theory categories. For instance, in the GBL study, some of the most frequent struggle codes centered on difficulties with generating ideas and communication within teams.
An additional category of codes encompasses aspects of learner experiences which students found beneficial, enjoyable, generative, transformative, and so on. Not only can these facets of experiences assist us in grasping what components of the design features were effective, but they can also be understood in their relationships with problematic aspects of learning - thereby enabling us to leverage strengths to remedy related weaknesses. In the generative GBL study, salient codes in the “what worked well” category included learner agency and a sense of joy.
After completing the coding process, the codebook often requires refining. Any codes used 3 or fewer times are either merged together with other closely related codes or deleted entirely. We also assess codes applied much more frequently than others, analyze all segments assigned that code, and seek to split it into two or more narrower codes.
During the codebook development phase, the research team meets regularly to negotiate differences in coding interpretations and applications. It is also advisable to perform inter-rater reliability testing once the codebook is nearly finalized and during the coding process, continuing to resolve discrepancies until the team achieves their target level of consistency and reliability. After this refining process, the finalized codebook for the GBL study encompassed 56 theory codes, 10 struggle codes, and 16 codes for aspects that worked particularly well for students.
LENA: Co-occurrence analysis
Next, analysis is conducted to determine the likelihoods that pairs of codes co-occur, signifying interrelatedness between the coded aspects of learning experiences. In our own work, we utilize Pearson’s correlations. We calculate correlations for all possible pair combinations among codes in the three categories (theory, what worked, struggles) and export the results as a Microsoft Excel file containing the symmetrical correlation matrix. We have also employed other analytical techniques such as conducting code co-occurrence frequency analysis, which is beneficial for studies involving longer individual text documents like interviews or journals. This allows us to constrain the parameters to include only code co-occurrences within a few sentences or paragraphs rather than across an entire text document.
The Excel files generated by the qualitative analysis software require transformation before they can be successfully imported into the network analysis program. The symmetrical correlation matrix has the names of all the codes as column and row headers. Each cell in the matrix specifies the Pearson’s r value and the p-value designating whether that correlation is statistically significant. Therefore, we create three duplicated sheets representing the p < .001, p < .01, and p < .05 significance levels. With some cleaning and manipulation, each sheet contains only the significant Pearson's r values at that confidence level, with all other cells left blank.
LENA: Network analysis

We then import these cleaned Excel data into the network analysis software to construct separate network database files for each significance level (p < .001, p < .01, and p < .05). Next, we generate the network maps configured as 1-mode networks (see figure 1). We always start with the most stringent significance level, p < .001, and gradually work our way down if that initial map is too sparse or disconnected. Often the p < .001 network consists of dyads and triads (two or three connected nodes), so we switch to p < .01 to achieve a map with greater connectivity among numerous nodes. Our goal is generally to have all nodes linked together in one unified network map in order to best comprehend the complexity of the interrelationships. Consequently, any nodes not connected to the main network are deleted, since we are only interested in analyzing clear patterns of interdependencies between the different aspects of the learning experience. If there are triads or larger groupings that are disconnected from the primary network and do not contain any struggle codes, these nodes are typically removed as well.

Our subsequent step is to conduct cluster analysis on the network map. We utilize the Girvan-Newman algorithm (Girvan & Newman, 2002) given that it is the most commonly leveraged technique for community detection in networks. Girvan-Newman clustering operates by calculating measures of distance between each node, known as edge-betweenness, and incrementally removing the connections between nodes that have the highest betweenness. Edge-betweenness signifies how many shortest paths run along that edge between any given pair of nodes. The higher the edge-betweenness value for a tie between two nodes, the greater prominence that connection has regarding information flow throughout the broader network structure. Edge-betweenness is recalculated at each step, and the process iterates until distinct non-overlapping clusters emerge.
We start with a minimum of two clusters and ultimately opt for the number of communities that produce the highest Q value while also not generating an unusable proliferation of clusters. Each cluster must contain at least one struggle code and at least one what worked or theory code to ensure meaningful interpretation can take place. Higher Q values denote greater confidence that the clusters represent substantive and non-arbitrary groupings within the network. We strive for Q values above 0.3 and avoid values dipping below 0.3 if possible

because values above 0.3 indicate a fairly strong confidence that the clusters represent group structures present in the data, and values above 0.5 indicate very strong confidence (Valente et al., 2015). In our GBL study, cluster analysis of the p < .01 network yielded three clusters at Q = 0.413. If the p < .001 map does not produce clusters containing the requisite node types or has an unacceptable Q value, we reject that level and examine the p < 0.01 network instead.
For studies encompassing a substantial number of codes, determining where to initiate interpretation of the network maps can pose challenges. To assist with this, we conduct node betweenness centrality analysis, which gauges betweenness centrality for each individual node as opposed to betweenness of the edges as in the previous step. We then adjust the node sizes so that nodes with greater betweenness centrality values appear larger. This highlights which nodes serve vital bridging roles connecting disparate portions of the network, and therefore can be conceptualized as leverage points within the broader system. Pinpointing these influential nodes aids researchers in deciding where to focus analytic attention when making sense of large, highly interconnected network maps. In our GBL study, cluster analysis resulted in a map of p < .01 with 3 clusters at Q = 0.413 (see figure 2).
LENA: Design moves
We create a table to methodically translate the network maps into actionable design moves with columns for: Struggles, What Worked, Principles from Theory, and Design Moves. Within each cluster, we list the struggle codes in the Struggles column. The What Worked and Principles from Theory columns are populated with codes from those categories that are closely tied to each struggle code based on the network map interlinkages. In the Design Moves column, we formulate targeted changes to the learning design aimed at addressing each struggle by harnessing the connected aspects that worked well and principles derived from relevant learning theories. During this process, we remain cognizant of potential ripple effects that a design move tailored to one cluster may trigger in other clusters representing different profiles of learner experience.

Once promising design moves have been devised for each cluster, the next step is to integrate those changes and implement the redesigned learning experience in the subsequent iteration. LENA was specifically developed to construct design modifications between cycles of design-based research rather than for evaluative purposes such as quantifying the impact of a given design. Each iteration constitutes a unique, multifaceted context. Consequently, confidence in precisely attributing observed changes from one iteration to the next to specific design moves is limited. However, conducting a greater number of iterative cycles may help strengthen claims about the influence of targeted tweaks.

Implications and discussion
LENA enables researchers to systematically identify patterns in learner-generated data about their learning experiences, providing actionable insights for iterative design improvements. The network visualizations make visible the complex interconnections between learner struggles, positive experiences, and theoretical principles, offering a holistic understanding of how learning designs are experienced in practice. It supports novice design- based researchers by offering guidance grounded in contextual evidence. Addressing “why” in addition to “what” and “how” questions is promoted through explicitly integrating relevant learning theory principles. We can gain insight into how learners experience our designs from both practical and theoretical perspectives. LENA serves as a bridge integrating fragmented individual experiences into a coherent picture to enable data-driven design decisions. We believe it empowers embracing the inherent messiness and complexity of learning without the researcher becoming overwhelmed. LENA also provides an opportunity to understand participants’ authentic experience by reducing potential research bias or problematic assumptions arising from lack of experience working with learning theories or human subjects data. Instead of relying on researchers’ own presumptions, LENA allows researchers to identify patterns in learner-generated data regarding their complex learning experiences.
In future work, we aim to pursue creative strategies for more meaningfully including students as co- design partners in DBR processes. This will enable tapping into learner perspectives in richer ways compared to solely relying on post-hoc reflections. LENA’s ongoing evolution has followed an extended developmental trajectory, but we now feel it is robust enough to share publicly to invite constructive critiques and refinements from fellow learning scientists. To further validate the LENA approach, future studies could compare the interpretations generated by novice researchers using LENA to those of expert researchers conducting more traditional qualitative analysis. Such comparisons would provide additional evidence of the reliability and effectiveness of the LENA system in enabling rigorous design-based research. Through this paper, we call for learning scientists to collaboratively advance LENA and other complementary methods to strengthen the sophistication of design-based research.
